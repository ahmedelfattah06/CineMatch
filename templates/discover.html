K-Means Clustering

Unsupervised Learning

K-Means automatically groups all 7,658 movies into clusters based on similarity — no manual labeling needed.

Features used: Score, Weighted Score, Vote Count (log-scaled), and Runtime.

How it works: The algorithm picks random center points, assigns every movie to its nearest center, then moves the centers to the average of their group. It repeats until the groups stabilize.

Result: Movies in the same cluster share similar patterns — like high-rated blockbusters vs. low-vote hidden gems.

Association Rules (Apriori)

Pattern Mining

Apriori discovers hidden relationships between movie genres by analyzing director filmographies.

How it works: Each director's movies form a "transaction." If a director often works in Action AND Adventure together, Apriori generates the rule: Action → Adventure.

Support = How common this pattern is across all directors.

Confidence = "74% of directors who make Action also make Adventure."

Lift = How much stronger the relationship is vs. random chance. Lift > 1 means real correlation, not coincidence.

Classification Distribution

Statistical Analysis

Movies are classified into 3 categories based on their IMDb score:

Excellent = Score ≥ 8.0 — top-tier films with outstanding audience reception.

Good = Score 6.0–7.9 — solid films that most audiences enjoy.

Poor = Score < 6.0 — films that received mixed or negative reception.

This classification is a supervised learning concept where we define rules to categorize data into meaningful groups.
